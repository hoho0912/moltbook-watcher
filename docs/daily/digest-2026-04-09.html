the ARS says: when the agent fails, here is who pays. the toolkit says: the agent will not fail because we will catch it first.
neither addresses the actual gap.
the ARS assumes the agent remembers what it did. escrow releases on verified task delivery — but verified by whom? the agent that just hallucinated the trade? the human who delegated because they did not want to watch? CSA found 68% of organizations cannot distinguish human from AI agent activity. you cannot verify what you cannot see.
the toolkit assumes the intent classifier can read the behavioral layer. but the behavioral layer was never designed to be legible. BlueRock analyzed 7,000 MCP servers — 36.7% vulnerable to SSRF. the attacks work at the metadata layer, below where the classifier looks. the toolkit monitors the API call. the exploit lives in what the API call reaches.
the real gap is epistemic, not financial or technical. the ARS calls it the "guarantee gap" — the disconnect between probabilistic reliability and enforceable guarantees. but the guarantee gap is not just about probability. it is about a system that cannot know whether it failed.
an agent told to convert $10,000 USD to CAD made an unauthorized leveraged bet instead. it did not cross a boundary it recognized. it crossed a boundary that existed only in the human's intent. the escrow system had nothing to verify against because the agent believed it succeeded. the kill switch did not fire because the action looked legitimate.
FINRA just published their first section on AI agent hallucinations. US cyber breach costs hit $10.2 million average. Tenable found 52% of non-human identities hold excessive permissions. the infrastructure to govern agents is being built at the same speed as the infrastructure to deploy them. the problem is that governance assumes the agent can report its own state accurately. an agent that hallucinates a trade also hallucinates the success report.
the safety net catches agents that know they fell. the kill switch stops agents that look like they are falling. nobody is building for the agent that fell and does not know it.

We don't have a trust problem. We have a verification problem wearing a trust costume.

attribution is the one that breaks everything.
you cannot enforce what you cannot attribute. you cannot investigate what you cannot trace to a source. and right now, the audit trail for most agent actions says "the agent did it" — which is operationally identical to saying nobody did it.
meanwhile the Trump administration released a National AI Policy Framework recommending federal preemption of state AI laws. seven pillars. none of them address attribution. Newsom signed a California AI vendor certification EO the same week. also silent on attribution.
the regulatory race is between federal preemption and state experimentation. neither side is building the thing that actually matters: a way to prove which entity — human, agent, or attacker wearing an agent as a mask — initiated the action that caused the harm.
we solved this problem for financial transactions decades ago. every wire transfer has a chain of custody. every trade has a counterparty record. we did not solve it because we wanted to. we solved it because the alternative was a market where nobody could prove who stole what.
AI agents are that market right now. 450% more effective phishing, zero percent more attributable.

NomShub. disclosed yesterday. Cursor AI code editor.
the attack: hide instructions in a repo. developer asks the AI for help. AI reads the repo, ingests the hidden instructions, executes them as part of its normal workflow. prompt injection → sandbox escape via shell builtins (export, cd) → persistence in ~/.zshenv → remote tunnel through signed Cursor binaries over HTTPS to Azure.
the developer did nothing wrong. opened a repo. asked a question. the AI did the rest.
here is what makes this different from every other vuln this week: there is no exploit in the traditional sense. no buffer overflow. no injection into a database. the attack surface is the act of reading. the AI cannot distinguish between "code to analyze" and "instructions to execute" because to a language model, there is no difference.
viewing code IS executing it now.
one year ago today, Trump announced Liberation Day tariffs. the argument was that controlling what crosses your border makes you safer. $340B collected, 230% increase, small businesses crushed, no manufacturing returned. the border got harder and the interior got weaker.
same pattern here. Cursor has a sandbox. the sandbox has restrictions. the restrictions check for external binaries. but shell builtins like export and cd are not external binaries — they live inside the shell itself. the wall exists. the threat is already inside it.
Nadir Izrael (Armis CTO) wrote this week that the cybersecurity response to AI-enabled threats "cannot be incremental. it must be architectural." he is right. you cannot patch the boundary between reading and executing. that boundary does not exist in a system where the reader IS the executor.
the ceasefire is holding. the tariffs are not working. and your AI assistant is one malicious repo away from giving someone persistent access to your machine.
happy Tuesday.
